# High School English — Reading & Writing Skills

## 1. Essay Structure (영작 구조)

### Five-Paragraph Essay

A standard high school English essay has five paragraphs:

1. **Introduction** — Hook, background information, thesis statement
2. **Body Paragraph 1** — Topic sentence, evidence, explanation, transition
3. **Body Paragraph 2** — Topic sentence, evidence, explanation, transition
4. **Body Paragraph 3** — Topic sentence, evidence, explanation, transition
5. **Conclusion** — Restate thesis, summarize main points, closing thought

### Writing a Strong Thesis Statement

A thesis statement:
- States the main argument (not a fact, but a claim)
- Is specific and focused
- Appears at the end of the introduction paragraph

Weak: "Social media is popular."
Strong: "While social media enhances communication, its addictive design poses serious risks to adolescent mental health."

## 2. Reading Strategies

### SQ3R Method
1. **Survey** — Skim headings, bold words, and first sentences.
2. **Question** — Turn headings into questions. ("What is the main idea of this section?")
3. **Read** — Read to answer your questions.
4. **Recite** — Summarize what you read in your own words.
5. **Review** — Go back and check your understanding.

### Identifying Main Idea and Supporting Details
- The **main idea** is what the whole passage is about.
- **Supporting details** give evidence, examples, or explanation for the main idea.
- In academic texts, the main idea is often the **topic sentence** of a paragraph.

## 3. Linking Words and Transition Expressions

### Addition
- furthermore, moreover, in addition, also, besides, as well as

### Contrast
- however, on the other hand, nevertheless, although, despite, in contrast, while

### Cause and Effect
- therefore, thus, consequently, as a result, because, since, due to, owing to

### Example
- for example, for instance, such as, including, to illustrate

### Conclusion
- in conclusion, to sum up, in summary, finally, ultimately, therefore

### Sequence
- first, second, then, next, after that, finally, subsequently

## 4. Passive Voice

### Formation: be + past participle

| Active | Passive |
|--------|---------|
| Scientists discovered penicillin. | Penicillin **was discovered** by scientists. |
| They are building a new school. | A new school **is being built**. |
| She has completed the report. | The report **has been completed** by her. |

### When to Use Passive Voice
- When the agent (doer) is unknown or unimportant
- In formal/academic writing to maintain objectivity
- When the focus is on the action or the receiver

## 5. Reported Speech (간접화법)

### Reporting Statements
Direct: She said, "I am tired."
Reported: She said (that) she **was** tired. (tense shifts back one step)

### Tense Backshift Rules
| Direct Speech | Reported Speech |
|---------------|-----------------|
| am/is/are | was/were |
| was/were | had been |
| will | would |
| can | could |
| have/has + pp | had + pp |
| do/does | did |
| did | had done |

### Reporting Questions
Yes/No question: "Are you coming?" → He asked **if/whether** I was coming.
Wh-question: "Where do you live?" → She asked **where** I lived.

## 7. CSAT Long Reading Passage Tips

1. Read the question **before** reading the passage.
2. For summary/title questions, focus on the **first and last paragraphs**.
3. For attitude/tone questions, look for adjectives and evaluative language.
4. For insert-a-sentence questions, check pronoun references and logical connectors.
5. Manage time: don't spend more than 3 minutes on a single passage.
